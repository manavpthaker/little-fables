# Azi and the Thunder Symphony

*A Musical Journey Through Feelings*

Chapter 1: When the Music Won't Work

Azi sat cross-legged on his circular rug, fingers pressing the same three chords on his AKAI keyboard over and over. C-G-Am. C-G-Am. The sound echoed through his playroom, but something felt terribly wrong. Each note came out sharp and angry, like the music itself was arguing with him.

"Why won't you work?" he muttered, his frustration building like steam in a kettle. His friends were scattered around the rug—Jujy with his confident black-and-white stripes, Dory looking dreamy and soft, and gentle Citie sitting quietly nearby, always ready to listen.

The morning light streamed through the window, but Azi couldn't feel its warmth. All he could feel was the tangled mess of feelings in his chest—feelings that wanted to burst out like fireworks but had nowhere to go. His hands pressed harder on the keys. The chords crashed together like thunder clouds colliding in a storm.

"Maybe if I play louder," Azi thought, and he pounded the keys with more force. BANG. CRASH. CLANK. But the music only sounded worse—harsh and jagged, like broken glass.

That's when the hot feeling took over completely. Before he could stop himself, before he could think or breathe or count to three, Azi's hand swept across the keyboard and beyond—SMACK!—striking gentle Citie, who tumbled backward with a small, surprised cry.

The playroom fell silent except for one deep, echoing note hanging in the air like a question mark.

Azi stared at his hands, then at Citie's wide eyes. His friend's bottom lip trembled, and there was something new in his expression—something that looked like hurt mixed with confusion. "Citie..." Azi whispered, feeling his anger drain away and leave behind something cold and heavy in its place.

"I... I didn't mean..." But the words stuck in his throat like peanut butter.

Chapter 2: The Note That Changed Everything

That single note from the keyboard—deep and resonant like the lowest string on Papa's guitar—began to grow and shimmer in ways that music shouldn't. It spread through the air like ripples in a pond, making the walls of the playroom fade and blur at the edges.

Azi blinked hard, rubbing his eyes, but the shimmering only grew stronger. The familiar sounds of his house—Mama humming in the kitchen, the neighbor's dog barking—grew distant and muffled, as if he were hearing them from underwater.

"What's happening?" he asked, but his voice sounded far away, even to himself.
