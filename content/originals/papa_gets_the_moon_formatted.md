# Papa Gets the Moon

*for Azi, who asked — and so I did*

*A bedtime story for sleepy eyes*

Part One: The Ask

One night, just before sleep, Azi stood at his bedroom window.

The day had been long and full. There had been breakfast with Mama, where the eggs were just right and the juice was cold and sweet. There had been playing in the backyard, where the tomatoes were getting redder and the bees were humming their slow summer songs. There had been music on the keyboard, and puzzles on the rug, and a story with Papa before the bath.

Now the house was quiet.

Azi's hair was still a little damp. His pajamas were soft. His feet were bare on the cool wooden floor.

And outside his window, the moon was rising over Rahway.

It came up slowly, the way moons do—like it had all the time in the world. First just a glow behind the rooftops. Then a sliver of light. Then more and more, until there it was: round and bright and beautiful.

"Papa," Azi whispered. "Look."

I came to stand beside him. Together we watched the moon float higher, leaving the rooftops behind.

"Can you get it for me?" he asked. "It's glowing so nicely. I want to hold it."

I looked at the moon.

It was big.

It was round.

It was very, very far.

But Azi was looking at me with those eyes—the ones that believe Papa can do anything.

"I'll try," I said.

And I meant it.

Part Two: The Ladder

First, I needed to get high.

I went to the garage and found my tallest ladder—the wooden one that smells like summer and sawdust, the one I use to trim the peach tree and clean the gutters and hang the holiday lights every December.

I carried it out into the night.

The neighborhood was quiet. Porch lights glowed here and there like little golden eyes. Somewhere a dog barked once, then settled. The air smelled like cut grass and somebody's late dinner.

I walked past Flo's house, where her garden was full of flowers that looked silver in the moonlight.

I walked past the fire station, where the big red trucks were sleeping in their bays. Even fire trucks need to rest.

I walked past the library, with its dark windows full of waiting stories. All those books, dreaming on their shelves.

I walked past Brady and Anne's house, where the porch light glowed soft and warm. Their cat was sitting in the window, watching me with curious eyes. I waved. The cat blinked slowly, which is how cats wave back.

Finally, I found the perfect spot: the old oak tree at the corner of our street. Its branches reached up and up like arms trying to touch the sky.

I set my ladder against the trunk.

I began to climb.

Up... up... up...

Past the first branches, where a bird had tucked her head under her wing for the night.

Past the middle branches, where a squirrel was curled in his nest of leaves, dreaming of acorns.

Past the highest branches, where the leaves whispered secrets to the wind.

I climbed until the ladder ran out of rungs.

I reached up with both hands.

I stretched as tall as I could stretch.

I tried.

But the moon just floated there, high and quiet, watching me with what might have been a gentle smile.

Not yet, it seemed to say. Not that way.

I climbed back down, rung by rung, thinking.

"I'll try again," I said to the moon.

And I meant it.

(Take a slow breath here... feel your shoulders relax...)

Part Three: The Tower

If I couldn't climb high enough, maybe I could build high enough.

I went back home and into Azi's playroom.

Jujy was there, sitting on the circular rug like he was guarding everything. His black and white fur caught the moonlight from the window.

"What are you doing, Papa?" Jujy seemed to ask with his confident eyes.

"Building a tower," I said. "To reach the moon."

Jujy tilted his head, thinking about this. Then he gave a little nod, as if to say: Sounds like a plan. I'll supervise.

I started with the books—Azi's favorites, the ones he asks for again and again. The ones in English and the ones in Spanish. The heavy ones about dinosaurs and trucks and how things work. The small ones about feelings and friends and finding your way.

On top of the books, I stacked his puzzles. The one with the animals. The one with the solar system. The one with all the colors that Azi can do faster than anyone.

On top of the puzzles, I added his magic tricks—the cups and balls, the disappearing coin, the wand that never quite does what it's supposed to but always makes everyone laugh.

On top of those, the red fire trucks. The little guitar. His AKAI keyboard that he's learning, one patient note at a time.

And on top of everything, every pot and pan and wooden spoon from his play kitchen—because sometimes the silliest things end up being the most important.

The tower wobbled. It swayed. It looked like it might fall at any moment.

But it held.

Because it was built with love. And love has a way of holding things together.

I carried the whole wobbling tower outside, one careful step at a time.

I set it down in the backyard, next to the garden where the tomatoes were sleeping and the basil was dreaming of being pesto.

And I climbed.

Up... up... up...

Past the books that told stories. Past the puzzles that taught patience. Past the music that was learning to sing.

I reached the very top, standing on a wooden spoon balanced on a pot balanced on a keyboard balanced on love.

I reached up with both hands.

I stretched as tall as I could stretch.

I tried.

But the moon just floated there, beautiful and patient, glowing its soft silver glow.

Not yet, it seemed to say. Not that way either.

I climbed back down, one wobbly step at a time, thinking harder.

"I'll try once more," I said to the moon.

And I meant it.

(Another slow breath... let your hands go soft...)

Part Four: The Song

I sat down in the backyard, next to my tower of love, and thought.

Climbing hadn't worked. Building hadn't worked. What else was there?

The night was quiet around me. The bees were asleep in their hives. The honeysuckle was breathing out its sweet smell. Somewhere, a cricket was singing its tiny cricket song.

Singing.

I thought about how Azi sings in the bathtub, with bubbles on his chin and both arms reaching up to the ceiling. How his voice gets louder and louder until the whole bathroom is full of music.

I thought about his favorite lullaby—the one about tomorrow, about the sun coming out, about betting your bottom dollar.

Maybe, I thought, the moon doesn't need me to reach it.

Maybe it needs me to call it.

So I sang.

Softly at first, just humming the tune. Feeling a little silly, sitting alone in the backyard, singing to the sky.

But then I thought of Azi waiting at his window, believing I could do this.

And I sang louder.

I sang the way Azi sings it—with my whole heart, with both arms reaching up, with everything I had.

The sun'll come out tomorrow...

And something changed.

The moon, which had been floating so high and far, seemed to pause.

It tilted, just a little, like someone leaning in to hear better.

I kept singing.

Bet your bottom dollar that tomorrow, there'll be sun...

The moon began to drift down.

Slowly. Carefully. Like a soap bubble floating on a breeze.

Past the stars, which twinkled their encouragement.

Past the wispy clouds, which parted to let it through.

Past the top of the oak tree, where the sleeping bird opened one eye, then closed it again.

Down... down... down...

Until it was right there.

Right in front of me.

Glowing.

I held out my arms, and the moon floated gently into them.

It was lighter than I expected. Warm, like it had been sitting by a fire. And it hummed—a soft, golden hum that felt like being hugged from the inside.

The moon had been waiting, I realized.

Waiting for someone to ask the right way.

Waiting for a song.

(Let your breath get slower now... like the moon drifting down...)

Part Five: The Journey Home

Now I had to get it home.

I held the moon carefully, the way you hold something precious. The way you hold a sleeping kitten, or a glass of water filled to the very top, or a secret you don't want to spill.

I walked slowly through the backyard.

The garden seemed to glow as I passed. The tomatoes looked silver. The basil leaves sparkled. Even the kale, which nobody thinks is beautiful, looked like it was made of moonlight and magic.

I walked out to the street.

Everything was different now, bathed in the moon's gentle glow.

I passed Brady and Anne's house. The cat was still in the window, but now its eyes were wide with wonder. Even cats don't see the moon carried home every night.

I passed the library, and I could have sworn the books inside were pressing against the windows to see.

I passed the fire station. One of the big red trucks seemed to blink its headlights at me—just once, softly—like a wink between friends.

I passed Flo's garden, where the silver flowers had turned to gold in the moon's close light.

Step by careful step, I made my way home.

The moon hummed in my arms.

Home.

(Slow down now... voice getting softer...)

Part Six: The Room Full of Friends

I tiptoed through the house.

Past the kitchen, where the dishes were clean and the counters were quiet.

Past the playroom, where Jujy was still on the circular rug, keeping watch.

Up the stairs, where each step creaked its familiar creak.

To Azi's room.

I pushed open the door, very slowly, very quietly.

There he was.

Azi was curled in his bed, that way he always curls—with his legs tucked up and his arms wrapped around something soft.

Pooh was right there with him, golden and worn and perfect. Pooh, who tends his honey farm and speaks like dusk and knows songs older than words.

Clappy was nestled close by, red and round. Even in sleep, he looked like he might start singing at any moment.

Monkie was there too, in his footie pajamas with all the pockets. A planner even in dreams, probably dreaming of lists and things to fix.

And on the chair beside the bed, Pandies sat calm and steady—the wise panda who once belonged to me, and now belonged to Azi, and would always belong to both of us. Citie was next to him, quiet and watchful, noticing everything even in the dark.

They were all there. All his friends. All his guardians. All waiting.

I crept to the little wooden bench by Azi's bed.

And there, very gently, I placed the moon.

It wasn't as big as it had been in the sky. Somehow, in the journey home, it had become just the right size—small enough to fit on a bench, big enough to fill a room with light.

Not a toy. Not a lamp. Not a pretend moon.

The real moon.

Glowing soft and steady.

Humming its quiet hum.

(Whisper-soft now... barely above breath...)

Part Seven: Almost Awake

Azi stirred.

Just a little. Just enough.

His eyes opened, soft with almost-sleep. They found the glow on the bench beside him.

Then they found me.

"You got it," he whispered.

"I got it," I whispered back.

"You really got it."

"I really did."

He smiled—that sleepy, satisfied smile that means everything is right.

"Was it hard?" he asked.

I thought about the ladder that wasn't tall enough. The tower that wobbled with love. The song that finally worked.

"A little," I said. "But you're worth it."

He reached out one small hand and touched the moon's glow. His fingers passed right through it—not like it wasn't there, but like it was made of something softer than soft. Something between light and love.

"It's warm," he said.

"It is."

"And it hums."

"It does."

"Like a lullaby."

"Exactly like a lullaby."

Part Eight: The Goodnight

I sat on the edge of his bed.

The moon glowed beside us, patient and kind.

Pooh and Clappy and Monkie were warm against Azi's side. Pandies and Citie watched from their chair, steady as always.

"One more hug," I said.

He wrapped his sleepy arms around me, the way he does every night. The way I hope he always will, even when he's bigger than me.

"Good night," I whispered.

"Good night," he whispered back.

"Sweet dreams."

"Sweet dreams."

"I'll see you in the morning."

"See you in the morning."

"I love you to infinity and beyond."

"Love you to infinity and beyond."

His eyes were already closing. His breath was already slowing. His arms loosened around me, going soft with sleep.

(Barely a whisper now...)

Part Nine: The End of the Day

And as he drifted off to sleep...

the moon sat quietly on its bench...

glowing soft and steady...

humming its quiet lullaby...

watching over Pooh, who dreams of honey and slow songs...

watching over Clappy, who dreams of music and joy...

watching over Monkie, who dreams of plans and fixing things just right...

watching over Pandies and Citie, who dream of quiet wisdom and small things noticed...

watching over Azi...

who asked for the moon...

and got it.

Because that's what happens...

when someone loves you...

enough to climb...

and build...

and sing...

until the sky gives in.

The room was full of golden light.

The room was full of sleeping friends.

The room was full of love.

And the moon...

the moon just glowed...

as if it had always belonged...

right there.

Right here.

With us.

**The End**

For the Reader

This story is designed for 10-15 minutes of reading. It has three movements: Adventure (engaging), Journey (transitional), and Rest (sleep-inducing). Your voice should naturally slow through each phase.

The story keeps interest through sensory details, familiar places, and the plush companions' involvement—while progressively deepening the calm. By the final pages, you should be barely whispering.

Breathing cues are optional. Interactive moments are marked—use them early in the story, then let the rhythm take over as sleep approaches.

Reading Guide for Parents

Timing

Total reading time: 10-15 minutes depending on pace and pauses. The story is designed to take longer than it needs to—the extra time is part of the settling.

The Three Movements

ADVENTURE (Parts 1-4): The Ask, The Ladder, The Tower, The Song. This is the engaging section—keep your voice warm and interested. Allow interactive moments here.

JOURNEY (Part 5): The walk home. Begin slowing here. Voice gets quieter. The world is settling around Papa.

REST (Parts 6-8): The bedroom, the waking, the goodnight, the close. Pure sleep territory. By the final page, you should barely be whispering.

Keeping Interest Without Energizing

The story stays engaging through sensory details (smells, textures, sounds) rather than action or excitement. This creates immersion without stimulation.

The familiar places (fire station, library, Brady and Anne's) let Azi participate through recognition rather than alertness.

Each plush companion gets a moment—this invites Azi to snuggle closer to his own friends without needing to respond.

Interactive Moments (Early Story Only)

What else should go on the tower? What do you think will work?

Can you hum the song with me? (Just for a moment)

After The Song section, stop inviting interaction. Let the rhythm carry him down.

The Echoing Goodnight

The goodnight ritual in Part Eight mirrors your real routine. If Azi is still awake enough, he can echo each line. If he's already drifting, read it as a gentle solo. Either way, it signals: this is real sleep time now.

If He's Still Awake

The final section (The End of the Day) can be repeated, even slower, as many times as needed. It's designed to be a gentle loop—watching over Pooh, watching over Clappy, watching over Monkie...

The Moon as Symbol

In the Azi-Verse, the moon connects Azi to everyone who loves him—Lito and Lita in Colombia, Dada and Dadi, family everywhere under the same sky. This story adds another layer: the moon is also what a parent will do for love. It's both cosmic and intimate. Both universal and just for him.

Sweet dreams.
