# The Midnight Train

*A Christmas Eve Dream*

Part One: The Sleeping World

The house had grown quiet. The kind of quiet that only comes on Christmas Eve, when even the walls seem to hold their breath.

Azi was already sleeping—deep, deep sleeping—curled in his bed upstairs with one arm around Jujy. His breathing was slow and even. His eyes moved gently beneath their lids, chasing dreams that hadn't quite formed yet.

Downstairs, the Christmas tree glowed like a tower of captured starlight. Beneath it, the little village slept too—tiny houses with painted windows, snow-dusted roofs, and lampposts that flickered with real warmth. The train tracks curved around them all like a silver river frozen in time.

The red train sat perfectly still on its tracks. It had been still all day, all week, all December. Just a toy train beneath a Christmas tree.

But Christmas Eve is different from other nights.

Somewhere far away—or perhaps very close—a clock began to chime.

One... two... three...

The sound traveled through the house like golden thread, weaving under doors and up staircases.

Four... five... six...

In his bed, Azi stirred. Not waking—not yet—but listening somewhere deep in his dreaming mind.

Seven... eight... nine...

Downstairs, the tree lights pulsed once. Just once.

Ten... eleven...

Twelve.

And the red train's lantern flickered to life.

It started small—the way all magic does.

A single light in a single window. Then another. Then the baker opened his tiny door, carrying a tray of bread no bigger than a fingernail. A lamplighter touched his pole to the streetlights, and they bloomed brighter. Children appeared in doorways, pointing at the sky.

The train's wheels began to turn—slowly at first, then with purpose. Its bell rang out: a single silver note that rose through the house like a wish.

And upstairs, Azi opened his eyes inside his dream.

Part Two: The Invitation

Azi found himself standing in the living room, though he didn't remember walking there. He was wearing his pajamas still, and his feet were bare on the soft carpet. Everything looked the same—the tree, the presents, the fireplace—but everything felt different. Bigger. More awake.

The village beneath the tree was alive.

Truly alive. He could hear tiny voices now, and the clop-clop of a horse-drawn carriage no bigger than his hand. He could smell gingerbread and pine and something sweet he couldn't name.

"There you are," said a voice.

Azi turned. A girl stood beside the Christmas tree—a girl his size, with dark braids and a white nightgown and eyes that sparkled like she knew a wonderful secret.

"I'm Clara," she said. "I've been waiting for you. The train only comes when someone is ready to believe."

"¿El tren?" Azi asked. "The train is real?"

Clara smiled. "On Christmas Eve, everything is real. Come on—we don't want to miss it."

She took his hand—her fingers were warm, like she'd been holding them near a fire—and together they walked toward the tree. With each step, the room seemed to grow larger around them. Or perhaps they were growing smaller. The tiny village was now a real village. The train was now a real train, gleaming red with golden trim, steam puffing from its stack in soft white clouds.

Its door stood open, golden light spilling like honey onto the snow.

The conductor stood beside the door. He was tall—the tallest person Azi had ever seen—with a long coat and a cap pulled low. But his eyes were kind, and when he smiled, his whole face crinkled like wrapping paper.

"Welcome aboard the Midnight Train," he said, his voice deep and warm like cocoa. "All aboard for the Land of Christmas Dreams."

"¿A dónde vamos?" Azi asked. Where are we going?

"Ah," said the conductor, tipping his cap. "That depends on you, young traveler. The train goes where hearts need to go. Tonight, it seems your heart has quite the journey planned."

Azi climbed the golden steps, Clara right behind him. Inside, the train car was beautiful—red velvet seats, frosted windows, lanterns that cast dancing shadows on the ceiling. Other children sat in the seats, children from everywhere, speaking languages Azi didn't understand but somehow felt he knew.

The train's bell rang three times. The whistle called out into the night.

And they began to move.

Part Three: The Journey

The train curved around the Christmas tree one last time—but the living room was gone now. In its place stretched a world Azi had never seen, painted in silver and blue and soft, soft white.

Snow fell past the windows like slow-motion stars. Mountains rose in the distance, wrapped in ribbons of cloud. Rivers of frozen moonlight wound through valleys where ice flowers bloomed in colors that had no names.

"First stop," Clara whispered, pressing her face to the glass. "The Forest of Sugar Plums."

The train slowed as it entered a forest unlike any forest Azi had imagined. The trees were crystalline—branches made of spun sugar, leaves of candied violet and amber. When the wind blew through them, they chimed like tiny bells.

Creatures moved between the trees: rabbits made of marzipan, birds with peppermint wings, a fox whose fur shimmered like cinnamon in sunlight.

"Can we get off?" Azi asked.

The conductor appeared beside them, holding two cups of something warm that smelled like chocolate and dreams. "The forest is for looking," he said gently. "Some magic is best kept at a little distance. But you may listen."

Azi listened. And he heard it—a music that seemed to come from everywhere at once. Soft flutes and dancing strings, the kind of music that made you want to twirl even when you were sitting still. It was the trees themselves, he realized. The whole forest was singing.

"Es hermoso," Azi breathed. It's beautiful.

"It is," Clara agreed. "The Sugar Plum Fairy planted these trees a thousand Christmases ago. Every year they grow a little taller. Every year the song gets a little sweeter."

(Let the music settle into your chest... breathe it in slowly...)

The train rolled on, and the forest gave way to a vast frozen lake that stretched to the horizon. The ice was so clear Azi could see stars reflected in it—but when he looked up, he saw stars in the sky too, perfectly mirrored.

"The Lake of Reflected Wishes," Clara said. "Everything you hope for lives down there, waiting to become real."

Dancers glided across the ice—elegant figures in white and silver, spinning patterns that left trails of glitter behind them. They moved like they were made of moonlight, leaping and twirling in perfect silence.

One dancer—a woman with a crown of snowflakes—saw the train and raised her hand in greeting. Azi waved back, and she smiled before spinning away into a leap that seemed to last forever.

"That's the Snow Queen," Clara whispered. "She dances every Christmas Eve to keep the ice strong enough to hold all the world's wishes."

Azi watched her until she was just a tiny figure in the distance, still spinning, still leaping, tireless and beautiful.

Next came mountains—great snow-capped peaks that touched the stars. The train climbed through clouds that tasted like vanilla when Azi breathed them in, higher and higher until—

"Look!" Clara cried.

A city appeared in the valley below, and Azi gasped. It was made entirely of light—not buildings with lights inside them, but buildings made of light itself. Towers of warm gold. Bridges of soft silver. Streets paved with gentle rose and amber.

"The City of Christmas Lights," the conductor announced. "Where every light that ever shone on a Christmas tree goes when the tree comes down. They live there forever, keeping the city bright."

Azi thought of his own Christmas tree lights at home. He wondered if some of them had been here, in this glowing city, before they'd found their way to his living room.

"Do the lights remember?" he asked. "Do they remember the families they belonged to?"

The conductor's eyes grew soft. "Oh yes, young traveler. Lights never forget the children who believed in them. That's what makes them shine so bright."

(Feel that warmth... like a hundred tiny lights glowing inside your heart...)

The train climbed one final mountain, and there—at the very top—stood a castle made of candlelight.

Not candles, but candlelight itself, shaped into towers and walls and windows that glowed with every color of flame. Orange and gold and that deep blue you only see at the very bottom of a candle, where the fire meets the wax.

"The Castle of Christmas Dreams," Clara said, her voice full of wonder even though she'd seen it before. "This is where the train stops. This is where the magic lives."

The train slowed. The bell rang once—soft and silver. The door opened, and a rush of warm air filled the car, smelling of cinnamon and pine and something else... something that smelled exactly like home.

Part Four: The Castle

They stepped off the train into a courtyard of smooth snow. Other children were there too—children who had ridden from other Christmas trees, other living rooms, other dreams. They looked at each other with knowing smiles, the way people do when they share a secret too wonderful for words.

The castle doors opened, and out stepped a woman who seemed to be made of moonlight and kindness. Her dress was the color of snow at twilight. Her crown was made of tiny stars. When she smiled, Azi felt warm all the way to his toes.

"Welcome, dreamers," she said, and her voice was like a lullaby. "I am the Christmas Queen, and this is my home. Tonight, it is your home too."

Inside, the castle was even more beautiful. A great hall stretched before them, with a ceiling so high it disappeared into soft golden clouds. Tables lined the walls, laden with every treat Azi could imagine: cookies shaped like snowflakes, cups of cocoa that never grew cold, fruits that glittered like jewels.

But the center of the hall was empty—a great circular floor of polished ice, smooth as glass.

"On Christmas Eve," the Queen said, "we dance. Every child who rides the Midnight Train is invited to join the Christmas Dance. It only happens once a year, and it only happens here."

Music began—not from anywhere Azi could see, but from everywhere at once. The same sweet melody from the Sugar Plum Forest, but fuller now, richer, like an orchestra hidden in the walls.

Clara took Azi's hand. "You don't have to know the steps," she whispered. "The music knows them for you."

And she was right. The moment Azi stepped onto the ice, his feet knew exactly what to do. He spun and glided and leaped, and he wasn't scared at all—he was flying, he was floating, he was part of the music itself.

"¡Estoy bailando!" he laughed. "I'm dancing!"

All around him, children danced with mice in tiny suits and soldiers made of wood and ballerinas whose tutus sparkled like fresh snow. The Christmas Queen herself joined them, twirling through the crowd, touching each child gently on the shoulder as she passed.

(Feel yourself dancing... light as air... happy as starlight...)

When the music finally faded, Azi's cheeks hurt from smiling. Clara was laughing beside him, breathless and bright-eyed.

The Christmas Queen approached them, and in her hands she held something small and silver—a tiny bell on a ribbon of midnight blue.

"Every child who visits the Castle of Christmas Dreams receives a gift," she said. "This bell will always ring for you, Azi. On cold nights when you feel alone, shake it once, and remember: you danced with the stars tonight. You rode the Midnight Train. Magic is real, and it lives inside you."

Azi took the bell. It was lighter than air, but somehow he could feel its weight—a good weight, like a promise.

"Gracias," he said. "Thank you."

The Queen smiled and kissed his forehead, and where her lips touched, he felt warm and sleepy and safe.

Part Five: The Return

"The train is waiting," Clara said softly. "It's time to go home."

Azi didn't want to leave—not ever—but somehow he understood. The magic of Christmas Eve was precious because it didn't last forever. It came once a year, like a gift you had to wait for.

They walked back through the snow to where the train waited, its lantern glowing soft and steady. The other children were climbing aboard too, some waving goodbye to mice friends and snowflake dancers, some already yawning, the magic settling into their bones like warmth.

The train ride home was quieter. Children curled into their seats, heads against windows, eyes half-closed. The conductor walked the aisles, tucking blankets around small shoulders, humming a tune that sounded like a lullaby.

Through the window, Azi watched the City of Lights fade into the distance. Then the Frozen Lake, with its dancing Snow Queen still spinning beneath the stars. Then the Sugar Plum Forest, its trees still singing their sweet, strange song.

Clara sat beside him, quiet now.

"Will I see you again?" Azi asked.

"Every Christmas Eve," she said. "The train always comes. And I'll always be here."

She squeezed his hand once, and her fingers were still warm—but gentler now, softer, like the end of a hug.

(Your eyes are getting heavy now... that's okay... the train knows the way home...)

The train rocked gently, back and forth, back and forth, like a cradle. Like Papa's arms when he carried Azi to bed. Like Mama's voice humming low and sweet.

The moon appeared in the window—the same moon that watched over all the children of the world. The same moon that had hung over the castle, over the lake, over the whole Land of Christmas Dreams. It seemed to wink, as if to say: I'll keep your secret safe.

Azi's eyes were so heavy now. The velvet seat was so soft. The train's rhythm was so steady.

Somewhere far away, he heard the conductor's voice: "Approaching home station. All dreamers prepare for arrival."

But Azi couldn't keep his eyes open anymore. He clutched the tiny silver bell in his hand and let himself sink into the warmth, the softness, the gentle rocking of the train...

(One last breath in... let it out slowly... feel yourself floating...)

Part Six: Morning

When Azi woke, he was in his own bed.

Sunlight streamed through the curtains, golden and gentle. Jujy was tucked under his arm. Downstairs, he could hear Mama singing and Papa laughing, and the smell of something sweet baking.

Christmas morning.

He lay very still for a moment, remembering. The train. The forest. The castle. The dance. Had it been real? Had any of it been real?

Then he felt something in his hand—something small and cool and impossibly light.

He opened his fingers.

The tiny silver bell lay in his palm, catching the morning light. And when he shook it—just once, just gently—it rang with the sweetest sound he had ever heard.

"Era real," he whispered. "It was real."

He smiled and pressed the bell to his heart. Then he jumped out of bed and ran downstairs, ready to tell Mama and Papa about the most wonderful dream—the most wonderful real dream—he had ever had.

In the living room, the Christmas tree still glowed. The stockings hung full and waiting. And beneath the tree, the little red train sat quietly on its silver tracks.

Perfectly still.

For now.

Buenas noches, mi amor. Feliz Navidad.

The train will always wait for you.

**The End**

Parent Guide

**ABOUT THIS STORY**

"The Midnight Train" combines elements from The Polar Express and The Nutcracker to create a sleep-settling Christmas Eve story. Azi begins already asleep, and the train's awakening at midnight draws him into a dream adventure—mirroring Clara's experience when her nutcracker comes to life. Your actual train set beneath the tree becomes the magical portal.

**READING TIME:** Approximately 10-12 minutes at a slow bedtime pace

**STRUCTURE:**

• Part One: Azi already sleeping, train awakens at midnight

• Part Two: Clara invites Azi aboard

• Part Three: Journey through magical lands (Sugar Plum Forest, Frozen Lake, City of Lights)

• Part Four: The Castle of Christmas Dreams and the Christmas Dance

• Part Five: Gentle return journey with progressive settling

• Part Six: Morning discovery—the bell proves it was real

**EMBEDDED SLEEP TECHNIQUES:**

• Clock counting (1-12) as hypnotic anchoring

• Train's gentle rocking repeatedly described as cradle-like

• Progressive relaxation through journey pacing (adventure → settling → sleep)

• Breathing cues woven throughout Part Five

• Permission language: "your eyes are getting heavy," "that's okay"

• Moon as recurring comfort symbol (Azi-Verse continuity)

**READING TIPS:**

• Parts 1-4: Read with wonder and energy—this is the adventure!

• Part 5: Begin slowing down, getting progressively softer

• Breathing cues: Pause and breathe together

• Final pages: Voice should be barely above a whisper

• If Azi falls asleep before the end, that's perfect—skip to Part Six in the morning!

**CHRISTMAS MORNING MAGIC:**

Hide a tiny silver bell (with a blue ribbon if possible) under Azi's pillow or in his hand before he wakes. The story ending describes exactly this discovery—making the dream feel real and creating a cherished keepsake.

**SPANISH GLOSSARY:**

• "Buenas noches" (BWAY-nas NO-chess) = Good night

• "¿El tren?" (el TREN) = The train?

• "¿A dónde vamos?" (ah DOHN-day VAH-mos) = Where are we going?

• "Es hermoso" (es air-MOH-so) = It's beautiful

• "¡Estoy bailando!" (es-TOY buy-LAHN-doh) = I'm dancing!

• "Gracias" (GRAH-see-ahs) = Thank you

• "Era real" (EH-rah reh-AHL) = It was real

• "Mi amor" (mee ah-MOR) = My love

• "Feliz Navidad" (fay-LEES nah-vee-DAHD) = Merry Christmas

**NUTCRACKER CONNECTIONS:**

• Clara as guide (direct Nutcracker reference)

• Midnight awakening under the Christmas tree

• Sugar Plum Forest (Sugar Plum Fairy's domain)

• The Snow Queen dancers

• The Castle/Kingdom destination

• The Christmas Dance (Grand Pas de Deux equivalent)

• Waking to find it "might have been real"

**POLAR EXPRESS CONNECTIONS:**

• The magical train journey

• The tall, kind conductor

• Hot chocolate and velvet seats

• Children from everywhere gathering

• The silver bell as a token of belief

• "The train goes where hearts need to go"
