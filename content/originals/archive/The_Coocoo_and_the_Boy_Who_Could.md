The Coocoo

and the Boy Who Could

An Azi-Verse Story

For all the children who already know what to do

Part One: The House at Midnight

The Westfield was the kind of house that talked at night.

Not in words. In the language that houses use --- the creak of a
floorboard settling, the tick of a baseboard heater cooling down, the
way the wind came around the corner of the backyard and pressed its palm
against the kitchen window, just checking.

Mama had gone to bed. Papa had gone to bed. Even the garden outside had
gone to bed --- the kale tucked under its shadow, the beans done with
their climbing, the basil holding very still the way basil does when the
air gets cool and dark.

In Azi\'s room, the moon looked in through the window the way it always
did. Patient and round and unbothered. It had been watching over
children for a very long time. It knew how to wait.

Jujy was on the little sofa, slumped sideways the way a cat who means
business sleeps when no business is happening. Dory was beside him,
facing the wall, deep in whatever dreamy place Dory went when the lights
went out. Pandies sat on his shelf, eyes closed, hands folded, wearing
that invisible calm the way he always did --- like patience was a coat
he\'d had for years and knew exactly how to put on.

Azi was not asleep.

He was lying on his back looking at the ceiling, which was doing the
thing ceilings do when you stare at them long enough in the dark ---
going soft at the edges, like it was made of something that couldn\'t
quite decide what it was.

He was thinking about the day.

\* \* \*

Part Two: The Day That Had Been Hard

It had started at breakfast.

Papa had put down the plate --- rice, beans, a little kale from the
backyard garden, the good deep green kind that Azi had actually helped
plant --- and Azi had looked at it and crossed his arms. His fork was
right there. His hands knew exactly what to do with a fork. He had been
using forks for years. But something in him sat down on the floor inside
and refused to move.

\"I can\'t eat it.\"

Papa had gone very patient the way Papa went patient, which was a
specific kind of patient --- not cold, not angry, just waiting, the way
you wait for weather to change. \"¿Puedes intentar, beta?\" Just try,
son. The Colombian and the Indian mixed together the way they always did
in this house, like two rivers that had given up being separate a long
time ago.

But Azi had not tried. He had sat with his arms crossed and let the food
get cold and felt, somewhere underneath the crossed arms, that he
actually was hungry, that the kale actually did look good, that his
hands actually did know exactly what to do.

He had noticed, in the corner of the kitchen, something dim. A shadow
that didn\'t belong to anything.

But he hadn\'t thought too much about it.

\* \* \*

Later there had been the shower.

\"I can\'t do it myself.\"

But Azi had done it himself before. Many times. He knew the order ---
hair first, then face, then the rest. He knew how long to let the water
run warm before getting in. He knew to count to thirty and then he\'d be
done. His body knew all of it without even thinking. And still something
in him sat down on the floor inside and refused.

The shadow in the bathroom mirror had been bigger this time. More solid.
It had Azi\'s own crossed arms.

\* \* \*

And then there had been the words.

The sharp ones. The kind that came out before Azi even knew they were
coming --- fast and hard, like something that had been wound too tight
and finally let go. He had felt the feeling before they came, that buzzy
too-bright feeling that lived in his chest and his jaw and the backs of
his hands, the feeling that meant something was building. And he had
kept going anyway. And the words had come out. And Mama\'s face had done
the thing it did --- gone quiet in that particular way, not angry, not
hurt exactly, but something more like careful, the way you go careful
around something that\'s learned to bite.

And the very second --- the very second --- the words had left his
mouth, Azi had felt the bad feeling. The one that came after. The one
that was worse than the buzzy feeling, because at least the buzzy
feeling had somewhere to go.

He had looked at the corner of the room.

The shadow had been very large.

\* \* \*

And finally --- bedtime. Azi twisting and flopping and saying \"I\'m not
tired\" even though his eyes were doing that heavy thing, even though
his body was practically falling through the mattress with the weight of
being done with the day. Even though every single part of him was
begging, quietly, to just stop.

\"I can\'t sleep.\"

But he could. He always could. He knew how. He just had to lie still and
let it come.

Something had settled at the foot of his bed then. Not heavy. Not loud.
Just\... present.

Waiting.

Part Three: The Coocoo

Now, at midnight, Azi looked at the foot of the bed.

It was there.

He could see it clearly now, in the way you see things in the dark when
your eyes have stopped fighting the darkness and started working with
it. It sat very still. It had a shape that was almost-Azi --- the same
hair, the same hands, the same small shoulders. But everything about it
was dim, like a drawing of Azi made by someone who had only heard him
described and never actually met him. Its arms were crossed. Its chin
was pointed at the ceiling. It was wearing his expression from
breakfast.

Azi had known about the Coocoo the way you know about things without
being told --- the way you know that the house is different when Papa is
worried even if no one says so, the way you know that a storm is coming
even before you see the clouds. Every child had one. Every child\'s
looked a little different. Yours was shaped exactly like the parts of
you that you were pretending not to have.

\"You\'re bigger tonight,\" Azi said.

The Coocoo said nothing. It just sat there at the foot of his bed, in
that particular way it had of sitting, like it had all the time in the
world.

\"I know why,\" Azi said. He sat up. Pulled his knees to his chest.
\"Because of today.\"

The Coocoo\'s crossed arms uncrossed --- just slightly. Like something
in it had been waiting for that.

(Take a slow breath in\... and out\... feel the quiet room around
you\...)

Part Four: What the Coocoo Showed Him

The room shifted.

Not all at once --- gently, the way dreams shift. The walls went soft at
the edges. And suddenly Azi was watching breakfast the way you watch
something from outside of it --- the kitchen, the plate, the kale from
the garden, Papa going quiet and patient, and Azi with his arms crossed.

He watched himself say \"I can\'t eat it.\"

And he could see --- now that he was on the outside looking in --- that
it wasn\'t true. He could see his own hands, hanging at his sides,
perfectly capable. He could see that his mouth actually was watering a
little at the smell of the beans. He could see the part of him that had
sat down on the floor inside and refused, and it was such a small part.
Such a stubborn, tired, small part. And all the rest of him had just
gone along with it.

\"I can\'t\" and \"I won\'t\" are not the same thing.

The voice of the Coocoo was strange. It was his own voice, almost, but
slower. Like his own voice heard through water, or through sleep.

Your hands know how to use a fork. Your body knows how to shower. Your
heart knows how to be kind. Every time you say \"I can\'t\" for
something you already know how to do --- that\'s when I get to come.

The scene shifted again. The shower now. Azi watching himself stand at
the bathroom door, saying \"I can\'t do it myself\" --- and even from
outside of it he could see that wasn\'t true, could see his own body
already knowing the order, hair first, face, thirty seconds, done. The
knowledge was right there in his hands. It had always been right there.

\"But sometimes I don\'t want to,\" Azi said, to the Coocoo, to himself,
to the quiet dark room that was listening. \"Sometimes it\'s hard even
when I know how. Sometimes the knowing-how doesn\'t feel like enough.\"

Yes. That\'s real. There\'s a difference between \"this is hard and I
need help\" and \"I know how but something in me is sitting on the floor
and refusing to move.\" You always feel the difference. You know which
one it is. You\'ve always known.

Azi was quiet. Because it was true. He did always know. The knowing was
always there, under the crossed arms, under the \"I can\'t\" --- quiet
and certain as the basil in the garden holding still in the dark. He had
just been choosing to stand in front of it.

(Breathe slowly\... in for four\... out for four\... feel your body
getting heavier and softer\...)

Part Five: The Sharp Words

\"What about the words?\" Azi said. \"I didn\'t plan those.\"

The room shifted a third time. Slower now. More careful.

He watched the moment from the outside --- not the moment the words came
out, but the moment before. The buzzy feeling in his chest. The
too-bright feeling behind his eyes. His jaw going tight. He watched
himself notice all of it and keep going anyway, the way you keep
touching something hot because some stubborn part of you isn\'t done
yet.

He watched Mama\'s face.

He had to look away.

The sharp words aren\'t the real thing. The real thing is the feeling
before them. The buzzy-bright feeling that means your body needs
something it doesn\'t have yet --- food, or water, or rest, or just
someone to be still next to. Your body has been trying to tell you. In
its own way. The only way it knows.

\"I know that feeling,\" Azi said. \"I felt it today before ---\" He
stopped.

Before the words.

\"Before the words.\" A beat. \"I could have stopped.\"

You could have said, \"I think I\'m getting too full of feelings. I need
a minute.\" Those words exist. You have them. Your mouth knows how to
make them. You just didn\'t use them.

A long quiet stretched through the room. Azi sat with it. Not
comfortable, not uncomfortable. Just true. The way things are true at
midnight, when there\'s no noise left to hide behind.

\"I\'m sorry about Mama\'s face,\" he said. Not to the Coocoo. Just to
the room. To the night. To wherever sorry goes when you mean it.

Something in the Coocoo shifted. Softened. Just a little. The crossed
arms weren\'t quite so crossed.

\* \* \*

Part Six: What Makes It Smaller

\"What makes you go away?\" Azi asked.

The Coocoo was quiet for a long moment. The moon moved slightly in the
window, or maybe the window moved toward the moon. The house did its
soft creak. Somewhere in the garden a night-thing went past, doing its
quiet night-work.

When you do the thing. Not perfectly. Not even happily. Just --- when
you pick up the fork because you already know how. When you step into
the shower because your body knows the way. When you feel the
buzzy-bright feeling coming and you say, before the sharp words, \"I
need a minute, I\'m getting too full\" --- every time that happens, I
get a little smaller. Every time you\'re kind from the inside --- not
because someone told you to be, but because you feel it there --- I get
smaller. Every time you try even when you don\'t want to, I get smaller.

I\'m not here to punish you. I\'m here because you forgot what you know.
And when you remember --- I have nothing left to be.

Azi looked at the dim sideways shape of the Coocoo at the foot of his
bed. His own crossed arms. His own stubborn chin. Made of all the
moments he\'d sat on the floor inside and refused to move.

He didn\'t feel scared of it. He felt --- something else. Something more
like understanding. Like recognizing something in a mirror that you\'d
been walking past without looking at.

\"I know how to do more than I act like,\" Azi said quietly.

Yes.

\"And I know when my feelings are building before they spill.

Yes.

\"And I\'m kind. I just --- forget, sometimes.\"

Everyone forgets. That\'s not the same as not knowing. You have always
known. You were born knowing. You\'re a boy who could --- you just have
to decide to.

(Feel the pillow beneath your head\... the blanket heavy and warm\...
your breath going slow, slow, slow\...)

Part Seven: The Morning

The Coocoo was very small by the time Azi finally closed his eyes.

Just a flicker. Barely a shadow. Small enough that the moonlight was
almost enough to wash it out entirely.

Not gone. It wouldn\'t always be gone. It would come back when he forgot
again --- when the \"I can\'t\" was really an \"I won\'t,\" when the
buzzy feeling built and he let it go sharp, when he stood in front of
what he already knew and pretended he didn\'t know it. That was the
deal. But tonight it was small enough that Azi could sleep.

And he did.

\* \* \*

In the morning, something was different.

Not big. Not dramatic. Not the kind of different you can point to. Just
--- Azi was sitting at the table, and Papa put down the plate, and Azi
picked up his fork. Without being asked. Not because he was performing
picking up his fork, not because he was making a point of it. Just
because he could, and the food was there, and he was the one who was
hungry, and his hands already knew what to do.

He ate three bites before anyone said a word.

Papa looked at him over his coffee. Something passed between them ---
not words. Just a recognition. The kind that happens in houses where
people pay attention to each other.

\* \* \*

Later, Azi felt the buzzy feeling starting. The too-bright, too-full
feeling behind his eyes. His jaw going just slightly tight.

He found Mama in the kitchen. She was cutting something, her back to
him, humming one of the songs she didn\'t know she was humming --- the
kind that came from so deep in her she didn\'t need to think about it.

\"Mama.\"

She turned.

\"I think my feelings are getting too full. I might need a minute before
they spill.\"

Mama put down the knife. She pulled Azi into her side, one arm around
his shoulders, solid and warm.

\"Gracias por decirme, mi amor.\"

Thank you for telling me, my love.

They stood there together in the kitchen for a minute. Just stood there.
The buzzy feeling moved through Azi like weather and kept going, out the
other side, without ever becoming sharp words. Without ever becoming
anything that had to be sorry.

\* \* \*

That night at shower time, Azi turned on the water.

He counted while it warmed up --- uno, dos, tres, cuatro, cinco. He got
in. He did his hair first, face, the rest, counting to thirty the way
his body already knew to do. He came out and wrapped himself in the big
blue towel and stood there in the steam like a small burrito with a
serious expression, which was the expression he always had when he was
doing something he was quietly proud of but didn\'t want to make a big
deal about.

At bedtime, Pandies sat in his spot on the shelf. Jujy and Dory were
where they always were on the little sofa. The moon looked in through
the window. The house did its late-night talking --- the creak, the
tick, the wind around the corner.

Azi looked at the foot of the bed.

Nothing.

Just the moonlight on the blanket. Just the good heavy feeling of a boy
who had done the things he already knew how to do.

He lay down. Let his body go heavy. Felt the pillow hold the back of his
head. Felt his breathing slow, the way it always slowed when he finally
stopped fighting it.

(In through your nose\... out through your mouth\... your body getting
heavy\... heavy\... perfectly heavy\...)

The moon watched over him the way it always does --- over all the
children, everywhere, who are still learning that the hardest thing and
the most important thing are usually the same thing.

Doing what you already know how to do.

\* \* \*

Buenas noches, mi amor.

The Coocoo is very small tonight.

You already knew how all along.

A Note for Papa and Mama

What the Coocoo Is

The Coocoo is not a punishment --- it\'s a mirror. It\'s shaped like the
part of Azi that knows how to do something but is pretending not to.
This distinction matters: the story doesn\'t say Azi is bad. It says he
forgot what he already has. That\'s a gentler and more accurate frame,
and it puts him in control of the solution --- because the Coocoo
shrinks when he remembers, and he\'s the one doing the remembering.

Before the Story

You can say: \"Tonight\'s story is about something called the Coocoo.
Every child has one, and it looks different for every kid. Let\'s find
out what Azi\'s looks like.\" No need to set it up as a lesson --- let
the story do that work.

After the Story

You can ask, gently and without expectation: \"What do you think your
Coocoo looks like tonight? Big or small?\" This gives Azi a way to
self-report how the day felt --- not as confession, not as judgment, but
as information you\'re curious about together.

In the Moment

When the hard moments come --- the crossed arms at dinner, the refusal
at shower time, the buzzy feeling building toward sharp words --- you
can say very quietly: \"I think the Coocoo might be getting bigger. What
do you think would make it smaller?\"

Then wait. Let him answer. He knows. That\'s the whole point --- he
always knew. Your job is just to help him remember that he knows.

The Three Things That Shrink It

1\. Doing the thing himself --- eating, showering, getting ready ---
before being asked twice. Not perfectly. Just doing it.

2\. Naming the buzzy feeling before it turns sharp: \"I think I\'m
getting too full. I need a minute.\"

3\. Being kind from the inside --- not performed, not coerced, but felt.

The Coocoo will come back. That\'s part of the deal. But each time it
does, Azi now has language for it, and a path through it, and the
knowledge that he already has everything he needs to make it small
again.
