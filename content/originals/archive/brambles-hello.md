# Bramble's Hello

*A 5-minute bedtime story*

---

In a forest where the moss grew soft and the streams sang to themselves, there lived a small brown bear named Bramble.

Bramble had a cozy den, a kind mother, and a tummy that was always pleased with berries. But there was something else he wanted — something he couldn't quite catch in his paws.

He wanted a friend.

So one morning, when the dew was still on the ferns, Bramble set out into the forest with his most cheerful *Hello!* tucked carefully in his throat.

He hadn't gone far when he saw a bunny nibbling clover by a fallen log.

"Hello!" Bramble called.

The bunny looked up. Her ears went tall. Her nose went *twitch-twitch-twitch.* And then — *whoosh* — she was gone, leaving only a little puff of dandelion seeds where she had been.

Bramble blinked. "I just wanted to say hi," he said to the empty clover.

His chest felt a little smaller. But he kept walking.

By the brook, he met a fox with a tail like an autumn flame.

"Hello!" Bramble called, gentler this time.

The fox froze. The fox stared. And then — *swish* — the autumn flame flickered into the bushes, and the fox was gone too.

Bramble sat down on a stone. His chest felt smaller still. A little voice inside him whispered, *Maybe don't say hello to the next one. Maybe it's safer to be quiet.*

But Bramble took a breath. He stood up. And he kept walking.

By a sun-spotted meadow, he found a deer drinking from a still pool. Her legs were thin as reeds, her eyes were dark as river stones.

It was harder this time. The little voice was louder. *What if she runs too? What if she runs too?*

Bramble's hello was almost a whisper. But he said it.

"Hello."

The deer heard. Her head jerked up. Water dripped from her chin. And then — *taptap-taptap* — she leapt into the trees, her white tail bobbing goodbye.

Bramble's heart sank all the way down to his paws.

*Maybe my hello is wrong,* he thought. *Maybe I'm too loud. Too big. Too bear.*

He hung his head and trudged on, watching his own slow shadow.

He didn't see the great brown wall of fur until he walked right into it.

*Oof.*

Bramble looked up. And up. And up.

A grizzly bear stood above him, taller than three Brambles stacked together, with paws as wide as dinner plates.

Bramble's whole body went *still.* His heart went *thump-thump-thump.* Every part of him wanted to run.

This was a *real* fear. Not the small worry of saying hello to a deer. A fear so big it filled the whole forest.

But Bramble's legs wouldn't move. So he did the only thing left to do.

He took a tiny breath. And he said it.

"H-hello."

The grizzly's ears went up. Slowly — so slowly — he sat down on the moss, until his eyes were almost level with Bramble's eyes.

"Hello, little one," the grizzly said. His voice was deep and warm, like the inside of a hollow log on a sunny day.

Bramble tried to speak again. He squeaked instead.

The grizzly smiled. "It's alright. Most everyone runs from me. The squirrels. The rabbits. Even the wolves, sometimes." He sighed a great big sigh that smelled like honey. "I used to think it was something I did wrong."

Bramble's eyes went wide. "They ran from *me* today," he whispered. "A bunny. A fox. A deer. I only said hello."

The grizzly nodded slowly. "Ah. You see, little friend — sometimes others are scared not because of *who* you are, but because they don't know you yet. Their hearts are small and quick. They run first, and wonder later."

Bramble thought about this. He thought about how his own heart had thumped a moment ago. How his own legs had wanted to run from *this* bear.

"Oh," Bramble said softly. "I felt scared too. Just now. With *you.*"

"And here we are," said the grizzly, "still talking."

Bramble laughed — a small, surprised laugh, like a bubble rising up. The fear inside him hadn't disappeared. But somehow, it had gotten smaller, just by him standing still in front of it.

They walked together back through the forest. The grizzly told Bramble his name was Mose. Mose liked watching clouds. Bramble liked his mother's humming.

And as they walked, an idea began to twinkle in Bramble's head, the way a firefly twinkles at dusk.

"Mose," Bramble said, "I think I know how to help the others not be scared."

---

✦ ✦ ✦

The next morning, in the clearing where the three paths met, a small wooden sign appeared. The letters were a little wobbly, because they had been carved with bear claws, but anyone could read them:

> **SCARE THE BEARS**
> *Today only. Free.*

The bunny came first, her nose going *twitch-twitch-twitch.* She was scared. But she was curious too, and curious was bigger than scared today.

Then came the fox, with his autumn-flame tail. His paws shook a little. But he came.

Then came the deer, stepping carefully on her reed-thin legs, her dark eyes wide.

In the middle of the clearing stood Bramble and Mose, with their backs turned.

The bunny looked at the fox. The fox looked at the deer. None of them had ever stood so close to a bear before. Their hearts were thumping. Their legs wanted to run.

But they stayed.

"BOO!" squeaked the bunny.

"BOO!" yipped the fox.

"BOO!" called the deer, who had never said *boo* to anyone in her whole life.

Bramble and Mose jumped. They flopped onto their backs in the moss. They wiggled their paws in the air and made silly bear sounds — *uh-oh-oh* and *ooh-ooh-ooh* — until the bunny was giggling, and the fox was rolling, and the deer was making the soft snorty laugh that deer make when no one is looking.

They played all afternoon. The bunny scared them from behind a stump. The fox scared them from inside a bush. The deer scared them from up on a little hill.

And every time they stayed instead of running, the fear got smaller. Until by the end of the day, it was so small that nobody could find it at all — only the laughter was left.

---

✦ ✦ ✦

That night, Bramble curled up in his den, his belly full of berries and his ears full of laughter from the day.

He thought about all the times he had been scared. The little voice that had told him to stop saying hello. The great brown wall of fur he had walked right into. The way his legs had wanted to run, every time.

And he understood something now, the way you understand a song after you've sung it through.

Brave wasn't *not* being scared.

Brave was saying hello anyway.

The moon slipped between the pine branches and laid a silver paw on his fur.

Bunny was asleep in her burrow. Fox was asleep in his hollow. Deer was asleep in her thicket of ferns. And big Mose was asleep under the stars, snoring like a faraway thunderstorm.

Bramble closed his eyes.

And the forest, very softly, whispered…

*Goodnight.*
